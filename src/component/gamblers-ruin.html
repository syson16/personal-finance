Gambler's Ruin

Why a fair game can still end badly

The player has a finite bankroll. In this simulation the win probability is 50%, so each flip looks fair in isolation, but the player cannot keep playing after the bankroll reaches zero.

This is why betting options needs a real edge. If the trade is only fair, finite capital is still a problem: a run of losses can force you out before the long-run odds have time to show up.

More context

That stopping point changes the game. Wins let the player continue, but a long enough losing streak can end the entire sequence. Once the bankroll is gone, there is no next round where probability can even out.

Edge is not just being right often. It has to survive leverage, expiration, sizing, and costs.

In options, the edge has to be large enough to survive the structure of the trade. Leverage makes small moves matter more, expiration can turn a nearly-right idea into a total premium loss, and bid-ask spreads or commissions can turn a fair setup into a negative one. A strategy can be intellectually correct and still be untradeable if the bankroll cannot survive the losing streaks.

This is the core idea behind gambler's ruin: fair odds do not remove the risk of ruin when one side has limited money. Survival depends not only on the probability of winning a single round, but also on bankroll size, bet size, and how many rounds the player keeps taking.
